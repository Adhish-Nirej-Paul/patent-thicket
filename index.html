Patent
Thicket

A patent thicket occurs when a single product is covered by so many overlapping patents, held by so many different owners, that bringing it to market requires navigating a dense web of licensing negotiations — raising costs, slowing innovation, and creating barriers to entry.

03 — Policy Implications

Why Patent Thickets Matter

$120B

Annual Licensing Costs

Qualcomm alone collected over $6B/year in patent royalties at peak. Across the industry, cumulative licensing costs in mobile technology exceed $100B annually.

250K+

Patents Per Device

Estimates suggest a modern smartphone implicates over 250,000 active patents. Clearing rights to all of them is practically impossible for new entrants.

9–10%

Royalty Stack

When every patent holder charges individually, royalty stacks can consume 9–10% of device revenue — a phenomenon called "royalty stacking" or "double marginalization."

↓ Entry

Barrier to Innovation

Startups cannot afford to license hundreds of patents. Patent thickets thus entrench incumbents and stifle disruptive competition, the opposite of the patent system's intent.

FRAND

Standard-Essential Patents

Some patents are "standard-essential" (SEPs) for protocols like 5G. Holders must license on Fair, Reasonable, and Non-Discriminatory (FRAND) terms — but disputes over what's "fair" still fuel years of litigation.

PAEs

Patent Assertion Entities

Non-practicing entities ("patent trolls") acquire patents solely to litigate. They thicken the thicket without contributing to innovation, extracting billions in settlements.

04 — History

The Smartphone Patent Wars

The smartphone patent wars were a series of ongoing intellectual property disputes between the world's largest technology companies fought primarily between 2009 and 2019 — though the conflict has never fully ended. At their peak, over fifty simultaneous lawsuits were active in more than ten countries, involving Apple, Samsung, Nokia, Qualcomm, HTC, Motorola, Ericsson, and Microsoft. The disputes covered design patents, software utility patents, and standard-essential patents (SEPs) across the full stack of smartphone technology.

Origins. The conflict has roots in the feature-phone era, when Nokia, Ericsson, and Qualcomm had already built enormous portfolios of cellular SEPs that every mobile device manufacturer was required to license. When the smartphone era began with Apple's iPhone launch in 2007, these incumbents saw a new class of extremely valuable devices entering their licensing universe. Nokia — which was simultaneously losing market share to iPhone — filed the first major smartphone patent lawsuit in October 2009, asserting ten patents covering GSM, UMTS, and WLAN standards. Apple's immediate countersuit with thirteen patents signalled that the new era of mobile computing would be fought as much in courtrooms as in the marketplace.

The Android Dimension. Apple's 2010 lawsuit against HTC — the first Android manufacturer — transformed the conflict from a bilateral licensing dispute into a structural battle over the smartphone operating system itself. Steve Jobs reportedly told his biographer that he was willing to spend every dollar of Apple's cash reserves to destroy Android, which he viewed as a stolen product. Google responded in 2012 by acquiring Motorola Mobility for $12.5 billion — a deal widely understood to be primarily a defensive patent acquisition, giving Google a stockpile of over 17,000 patents to deploy against Apple and Microsoft. Google later sold Motorola's handset business to Lenovo in 2014, retaining the majority of the patents.

The Qualcomm Dimension. Running parallel to the design-patent battles was a deeper structural dispute over Qualcomm's control of cellular modem IP. Qualcomm's business model — collecting royalties from every smartphone sold worldwide calculated on the device's selling price, not just the chip's value — generated billions annually but attracted intense regulatory scrutiny. The US FTC, the EU, South Korea's KFTC, China's NDRC, and Taiwan's FTC all opened antitrust investigations between 2015 and 2019. Apple's 2017 lawsuit against Qualcomm was not merely a bilateral dispute; it was an attempt to dismantle a royalty regime that Apple calculated was costing it approximately $1 per iPhone per year more than it should have paid.

Current Position (2020–Present). The core Apple–Samsung dispute ended in a global cross-license in 2018. The Apple–Qualcomm war ended abruptly in April 2019 with Apple paying approximately $4.5 billion and signing a multi-year chipset and license deal — just one day before the FTC v. Qualcomm trial was due to begin. The FTC won at district court in May 2019, but was reversed by the Ninth Circuit in August 2020 on the grounds that Qualcomm's conduct did not cause the kind of anticompetitive harm the Sherman Act was designed to prevent. The FTC dropped the case in 2021. However, the wars have not ended — they have simply shifted terrain. Nokia is now aggressively pursuing 5G SEP royalties from OPPO (leading to OPPO withdrawing from several European markets in 2022), Lenovo, and others. Ericsson sued Apple in 2022 when their license lapsed, seeking to renew at higher 5G rates. InterDigital continues to assert its SEP portfolio against OEMs globally. The battlefield has moved from touch-screen gestures and rounded rectangles to the invisible infrastructure of 5G connectivity — and with 6G standardisation already underway, the next round has already begun.
